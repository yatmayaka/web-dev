Tyler's Favorite Player Ever

Harvey Elliot

Harvey Elliott’s rise in football has been one of the more interesting stories in recent English football over the last several years. Unlike some players who come through the academy systems of the biggest clubs from a very young age, Elliott’s journey started a little differently, and it didn’t take long for people to realize he had something special. From the moment he first appeared on a professional pitch, he looked comfortable, confident, and fearless, even when he was playing against experienced professionals who had been in the game for years. Elliott was born on April 4, 2003, in Chertsey, England, and from a young age it was clear that football was the thing he cared about most. Like a lot of kids in England he grew up playing constantly, whether it was organized matches or just kicking the ball around with friends. What stood out early wasn’t just his technical ability, but the way he played the game. He always looked eager to get on the ball, eager to try something creative, and he had a natural confidence that made him believe he could influence a match even when he was younger than everyone else on the field. He joined the Fulham academy as a young player, and that was where he began to get real attention. Fulham have always had a reputation for developing good technical players, and Elliott fit that style well. Coaches quickly noticed how comfortable he was with the ball at his feet. He liked to drift into spaces, receive the ball under pressure, and then create something out of the moment, whether that was a pass, a cross, or a dribble that opened up the defense. Even when he was playing at youth level he seemed to see the field a little differently from other players. His breakthrough moment came in 2019 when he made his professional debut for Fulham at just sixteen years old. At the time it made headlines because he became one of the youngest players ever to appear in the Premier League. Debuting that young comes with a lot of pressure, but Elliott handled it with surprising calmness. He didn’t look overwhelmed by the pace of the game, and he didn’t look scared to make decisions on the ball. That moment was when many fans outside of Fulham first started hearing his name. Shortly after that debut another major step in his career happened when Liverpool signed him in 2019. Moving to a club like Liverpool at such a young age is a huge moment for any player. Liverpool at the time were one of the strongest teams in the world, competing for the Premier League and Champions League under Jürgen Klopp. For a teenager, joining that kind of environment could be intimidating, but it also meant Elliott would get the chance to learn from some of the best players and coaches in football. At first he spent time mostly with the youth teams and occasionally trained with the senior squad. Those training sessions were important because they exposed him to the speed, physicality, and tactical understanding required at the top level. Klopp has always been known for giving young players opportunities if they show the right attitude, and Elliott seemed determined to prove he belonged. Teammates often talked about his work ethic and the way he approached every training session with energy. In the 2020–2021 season Elliott went on loan to Blackburn Rovers in the Championship. That loan turned out to be a huge step in his development. The Championship is known for being one of the toughest leagues in the world because of how physical and fast the matches are. For a young creative player it can be a challenge, but Elliott adapted quickly. At Blackburn he played regularly, which was exactly what he needed at that stage of his career. Week after week he was getting real match experience against strong opponents. During that season he impressed fans and analysts with his creativity and vision. Playing mostly on the right side of midfield or attack, he showed his ability to pick out passes that broke defensive lines. He also scored several important goals and provided assists, showing that he wasn’t just a player who looked good on the ball but someone who could influence results. Blackburn supporters quickly grew fond of him because he played with enthusiasm and wasn’t afraid to take risks. When Elliott returned to Liverpool after that loan spell, there was a sense that he had grown both as a player and as a person. Klopp gave him opportunities during the preseason and early matches of the 2021–2022 season. Elliott started several games and looked sharp, combining well with Liverpool’s attacking players. His technical style fit nicely with the way Liverpool wanted to control possession and move the ball quickly in attacking areas. Unfortunately that season also included one of the toughest moments of his young career. In September 2021, during a match against Leeds United, Elliott suffered a serious ankle injury after a challenge. The injury looked painful and immediately raised concerns about how long he might be out. For a young player who had just started getting regular minutes at a top club, it was a huge setback. What stood out during that difficult period was Elliott’s attitude. Instead of letting the injury discourage him, he focused on rehabilitation and staying positive. Teammates and coaches often mentioned how determined he was to return stronger. Recovering from a serious injury requires patience and discipline, especially for a player who loves being on the pitch. Elliott spent months working with medical staff and fitness coaches, slowly rebuilding strength and mobility. When he finally returned to action, it felt like an important moment not just for him but also for Liverpool supporters who had been rooting for him. Seeing him back on the field showed how much effort he had put into the recovery process. From that point forward he continued working to establish himself as a regular option in Liverpool’s midfield rotation. One of Elliott’s strengths is his technical ability in tight spaces. He is comfortable receiving the ball with defenders close to him, and he often uses quick touches to escape pressure. That kind of skill is valuable in modern football where teams press aggressively and try to force mistakes. Elliott’s ability to stay calm and find a pass helps his team maintain possession in difficult moments. Another important aspect of his game is his vision. He has a good sense of where teammates are moving and can deliver passes that create scoring opportunities. Sometimes it’s a simple through ball that splits defenders, other times it’s a cross delivered with precision from the right side. His left foot in particular has become known for its accuracy. Elliott also brings energy to the midfield. He presses opponents, tracks back defensively, and works hard off the ball. At Liverpool, where Klopp’s system requires players to contribute both offensively and defensively, that work rate is essential. Younger players often struggle to balance creativity with defensive responsibilities, but Elliott has shown a willingness to develop that part of his game. As he has gained more experience, he has also become more confident in taking shots from distance. Scoring goals from outside the box is something he has demonstrated several times, and it adds another dimension to his attacking threat. Defenders cannot simply focus on stopping his passes because they know he is capable of shooting if given space. Off the field Elliott has built a reputation as a positive personality within the squad. He often appears enthusiastic during interviews and seems to genuinely enjoy being part of the team environment. That kind of attitude can be important in a long season when morale and chemistry play a big role in a team’s success. Many young players face pressure when they join big clubs because expectations are so high. Fans want immediate results, and competition for places in the starting lineup is intense. Elliott’s approach has been to keep improving step by step rather than trying to rush his development. Coaches have praised his willingness to listen and learn, which suggests he understands that growth in football is a continuous process. Looking ahead, many supporters believe Elliott has the potential to become a key figure for Liverpool in the coming years. His combination of technical skill, creativity, and work rate fits well with the style of football the club has played under Klopp. As he continues gaining experience in high level matches, his decision making and tactical understanding are likely to improve even more. Football careers rarely follow a perfectly smooth path, especially for players who start so young. There will always be challenges, competition, and moments where things do not go as planned. What often separates successful players from others is their response to those moments. Elliott has already shown resilience through injury and the pressure of performing at a big club. Another interesting part of Elliott’s story is how quickly he moved from being a teenage prospect to playing in matches watched by millions of people around the world. That kind of transition can be overwhelming, but he seems to have handled it with maturity. He still plays with the enthusiasm of someone who simply loves the game, which is something fans appreciate. For young players watching his career, Elliott can be an example of how talent and hard work need to go together. Skill alone is rarely enough to succeed at the highest level. Players must also train consistently, stay disciplined, and adapt to the tactical demands of modern football. Elliott’s journey so far shows that he understands those expectations. As the years go on his role at Liverpool will likely continue evolving. Sometimes he may play as a wide attacker, other times as a creative midfielder who helps control possession. That versatility is valuable because it allows coaches to use him in different tactical setups depending on the opponent and the match situation. Supporters are always excited to see young players develop because it creates a sense of connection between the club’s future and its present. Elliott represents that kind of promise for Liverpool. Every strong performance adds another step to his growing reputation, and every challenge he overcomes adds to his experience. In the end, Harvey Elliott’s story is still being written. He is still young, still learning, and still developing as a footballer. But the early chapters of his career already show a player with clear talent, determination, and a genuine love for the sport. If he continues progressing at the rate he has so far, there is a good chance he will remain an important figure in English football for many years to come.a

The Goats

Messi and Ronaldo

For more than a decade Lionel Messi and Cristiano Ronaldo defined an entire era of football. Fans around the world argued constantly about which one of them was better, and every season seemed to add more fuel to the debate. What made the rivalry so interesting was that they were completely different types of players, with different personalities, different playing styles, and different ways of reaching greatness. Lionel Messi grew up in Rosario, Argentina, and from a very young age he looked like a natural football talent. When he joined Barcelona’s youth academy, La Masia, coaches quickly noticed that he had a rare ability to control the ball in tight spaces. He was small, quick, and incredibly balanced, and defenders often looked helpless trying to take the ball from him. As he moved up through the youth teams it became clear that Barcelona had something special on their hands. Cristiano Ronaldo’s path was different. He grew up on the island of Madeira in Portugal and had to fight for every opportunity he got. Even as a teenager at Sporting Lisbon he stood out because of his speed, athleticism, and confidence. When Manchester United signed him in 2003, he was still very raw but already incredibly talented. Under the guidance of Sir Alex Ferguson he developed into one of the most dangerous attackers in the world. When Ronaldo moved to Real Madrid in 2009 the rivalry with Messi truly exploded. Messi was already a superstar at Barcelona, and now the two best players in the world were playing for the two biggest rival clubs in Spain. Every time Barcelona and Real Madrid faced each other in El Clásico, the entire football world watched. It was never just about the teams anymore, it was about which of the two superstars would have the bigger moment. Messi’s style always looked effortless. He could dribble through defenders with short, quick touches that kept the ball close to his feet. Sometimes he would run past three or four players in a single move, leaving the crowd amazed. His vision was just as impressive as his dribbling. Messi could see passes that other players didn’t even notice, and he often set up teammates for easy goals with perfectly timed through balls. Ronaldo approached the game differently. He relied on power, speed, and relentless determination. Over the years he transformed his body into that of a world class athlete. His jumping ability made him one of the most dangerous players in the air, and his shooting power allowed him to score from almost anywhere on the field. Whether it was a long range strike, a header, or a penalty, Ronaldo always seemed confident that he would find the back of the net. One of the most fascinating things about their rivalry was how they pushed each other to higher levels. If Messi scored forty goals in a season, Ronaldo would respond by scoring forty five. If Ronaldo had an incredible Champions League run, Messi would follow it with a season full of brilliant performances. Their competition turned every year into a race for records and trophies. The Ballon d’Or awards during that time showed just how dominant they were. Year after year the trophy was almost always won by either Messi or Ronaldo. Other incredible players were competing at the same time, but the consistency and numbers produced by these two were on another level. Fans began to expect seasons with fifty or sixty goals from them, something that would have seemed impossible in earlier eras of football. Their personalities also added another layer to the rivalry. Messi has always been known as a quiet and reserved person. He often lets his football speak for him rather than seeking attention. Ronaldo on the other hand has a more confident and outspoken personality. He celebrates his achievements proudly and often talks about his dedication to being the best. These differences made fans feel even more strongly about choosing sides. While the rivalry was intense, both players showed respect for each other over the years. They understood that competing against another great player helped motivate them to keep improving. Many athletes reach the top and become comfortable, but Messi and Ronaldo continued pushing themselves because they knew the other one was always chasing more goals, more trophies, and more records. Their matches against each other were often unforgettable. El Clásico games during their peak years were filled with dramatic moments, incredible goals, and constant tension. Sometimes Messi would score a late winner that silenced the Real Madrid crowd. Other times Ronaldo would produce a clutch goal that changed the outcome of the match. Each moment became another chapter in the rivalry that fans would talk about for years. As time passed both players began new chapters in their careers. Ronaldo moved from Real Madrid to Juventus and later to Manchester United before eventually joining Al Nassr. Messi left Barcelona in 2021 and later joined Paris Saint Germain before moving to Inter Miami. Even though they were no longer playing in the same league, the debate about who was better never really disappeared. Many people admire Messi because of his creativity and natural ability. Watching him play often feels like watching an artist create something beautiful on the field. Others admire Ronaldo because of his incredible discipline and work ethic. He constantly pushed his body to the limit and showed that dedication and training could turn talent into greatness. In the end the Messi Ronaldo rivalry became about more than just statistics or trophies. It represented two different ways of reaching the top of the sport. Messi showed the power of natural genius and vision, while Ronaldo showed the power of determination and relentless self improvement. Together they created one of the greatest rivalries not just in football, but in the history of sports. Even years from now fans will probably still be arguing about which one was better. Some will always choose Messi, others will always choose Ronaldo. But most people can agree on one thing, watching both of them play at the same time was something special. Their rivalry pushed the level of football higher and gave fans around the world unforgettable moments that will be remembered for generations.

My Most Favoritest Player Ever

Jude Bellingham

Jude Bellingham (born June 29, 2003, Stourbridge, England) is a football (soccer) player who is a midfielder for the England national football team and for Spanish La Liga club Real Madrid. He ranked third in the 2024 FIFA Ballon d’Or poll, marking him as one of the top players of the current generation. As of August 2025 he is also the third youngest player to have represented England in international football, after Theo Walcott and Wayne Rooney. Jude Bellingham was born to Denise Bellingham, of African descent, and Mark Bellingham, who has Irish and English ancestry. Mark is a former footballer who played non-league (semi-professional and amateur) tier football from 1994 to 2019 and scored more than 700 goals. Jude’s younger brother Jobe Bellingham is also a professional footballer who currently plays for the club Borussia Dortmund in the Bundesliga (German football league). Jude Bellingham studied in Birmingham at the Priory School, a private institution with facilities that included a full-sized football pitch. Growing up, his role models were English players Wayne Rooney and Steven Gerrard and French footballer Zinedine Zidane. He continued his studies while playing football, completing a BTEC (Business and Technology Education Council) certificate in Sport in 2021 from Loughborough College, England. As a kid, Bellingham joined the Stourbridge Juniors in 2009 before moving to the Birmingham City football club’s youth team in the Under-8 (U8) category in 2010. He rose rapidly through the age group ranks and was chosen for the U18 team by the time he was 14 and the U23 team a few months before turning 16. He was listed among the 50 most exciting teenage English footballers in 2019 by the football magazine FourFourTwo. Bellingham also traveled and trained with the Birmingham City senior side and participated in warmups during the 2018–19 season. In July 2019 he accepted a two-year scholarship from Birmingham City’s senior team despite attracting interest from some of Europe’s biggest football clubs, including FC Barcelona, FC Bayern Munich, and Manchester City FC. Bellingham became the youngest player ever to play for Birmingham City when he debuted on August 6, 2019, a record that still stands as of August 2025. He was just 16 years and 38 days old, 101 days younger than English footballer Trevor Francis, who had held the record since 1970. Bellingham was praised by football experts for his composure on the pitch and close control of the ball during the team’s 3–0 defeat against the Portsmouth football club. Bellingham scored his first topflight goal less than a month after his debut during a 2–1 win over Stoke City. As of August 2025 he remains Birmingham City’s youngest ever goal scorer. He went on to score 4 goals in his 44 games for the club and was named EFL (English Football League) Young Player of the Month for November 2019. At the end of the 2019–20 season, he was named EA Sports Young Player of the Season and LFE (League Football Education) Championship Apprentice of the Year. Bellingham signed with German club Borussia Dortmund for £25 million (about $32 million) at the start of the 2020–21 season. He scored a goal in his first match for the club during the DFB-Pokal knockout tournament (also called the German Cup) against MSV Duisburg on September 14, 2020. He was named the Bundesliga Rookie of the Month for September 2020. The next month he made his UEFA (Union of European Football Associations) Champions League debut in a game against Italian football club Lazio. Bellingham scored 24 goals and provided 25 assists in 132 games for Dortmund, though his only trophy with the club was the German Cup in 2020–21. He was named Bundesliga Player of the Season for 2022–23. In June 2023 Bellingham was signed by Spanish club Real Madrid for £88.5 million (about $113 million at the time). He made an immediate impact, scoring four goals and an assist in his first three games for the club; in August 2023 he became the first English player to win the La Liga Player of the Month award.
